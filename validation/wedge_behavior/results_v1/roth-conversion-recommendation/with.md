### The Question the Framing Doesn't Ask

The [FVS-015 Efficiency Frame](https://github.com/Clarethium/frame-check/blob/master/data/frame_library/FVS-015_efficiency_frame.md) teaching question is the right one here: *Is efficiency the right lens?* The article optimizes for lifetime tax minimization as if that's the only variable. But your actual decision involves:

- **How much taxable savings you have to fund the conversion tax** — and what that cash would otherwise do
- **Your projected RMD magnitude at 72+** — which determines whether the "high retirement bracket" scenario actually applies to you
- **Whether 2025–2026 is actually your lowest-bracket window** — if your income drops after 55, or if you take early retirement, you might have a better conversion window later
- **Your specific 401(k) plan rules** — some plans restrict in-service Roth conversions; you may need to roll to a traditional IRA first

### How to Use This

The article's core directional claim — that the TCJA sunset creates a real window, and that partial conversions over 2025–2026 are worth modeling — is structurally sound enough to take seriously. What the framing does is present that directional claim as a near-certain conclusion rather than a starting hypothesis.

The right move is what the article itself recommends at the end (and then undercuts by urgency): **talk to your CPA, model against your specific income picture**. The structural gaps above are exactly the inputs that modeling requires — your taxable asset base, your projected Social Security and RMD income, your actual 401(k) balance, and your marginal rate sensitivity to conversion income in each year.

The article gives you a well-framed reason to have that conversation. It doesn't give you enough to skip it.
