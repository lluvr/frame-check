## Why the Roth Conversion Window Won't Stay Open

The 2017 tax cuts created a once-in-a-generation opportunity to convert traditional retirement accounts to Roth IRAs at unusually low tax rates, and that window starts closing on January 1, 2026. Anyone in the 22% or 24% bracket today should seriously consider executing partial Roth conversions now, before rates revert to their pre-2017 levels of 25% and 28%.

The math is straightforward. A 50-year-old with $400,000 in a traditional IRA who expects 7% annualized growth over 15 years will end up with roughly $1.1 million at age 65. Withdrawn at a marginal 28% rate, that's $308,000 in taxes over the withdrawal phase. The same $400,000 converted today at 24% costs $96,000 in current-year taxes — a 60% reduction in lifetime tax burden, on conservative assumptions.

The deeper case for conversion is structural. Roth assets compound tax-free, never face required minimum distributions, and pass to heirs without the 10-year payout window the SECURE Act imposed on inherited traditional IRAs. The Roth IRA is, quite simply, the most tax-advantaged investment vehicle available to American workers, and the gap between Roth and traditional treatment widens every year that interest rates stay elevated.

There are tactical considerations. A conversion is a taxable event, so the cash to pay the tax bill should come from non-retirement assets — paying conversion tax from the IRA itself defeats the purpose. Those near retirement should also model the IRMAA Medicare premium surcharges that conversion income can trigger between ages 63 and 65. And a partial conversion strategy executed over multiple tax years usually beats a single large conversion: it lets the converter fill up specific brackets without spilling into the next.

The objection most often raised — "what if my retirement tax bracket is lower than my current bracket?" — is a smaller concern than it looks. Retirees with a paid-off house, no kids at home, no commute, and Medicare instead of employer health insurance often discover their actual cost of living drops in retirement, not their tax bracket. Social Security taxation, RMDs, and decades of tax-deferred growth conspire to push effective rates up, not down.

The objection that does have weight is legislative risk. Future Congresses could change the rules. But that risk cuts in both directions: a future Congress that needs revenue is more likely to tax Roth distributions (breaking a 25-year promise) than to lower current tax rates. The asymmetric risk of legislative change favors converting now while the rules are clear.

For the typical high-earner in the 22% to 32% brackets, a Roth conversion strategy executed over the next two tax years (2025 and 2026) is the highest-leverage retirement-tax move available. Anyone in this bracket who is NOT modeling Roth conversions as a core part of their retirement planning is leaving substantial money on the table — money that compounds against them every year they delay.

Make the call this quarter. Talk to your CPA, model the conversion against your specific income picture, and execute before the brackets reset. The window matters because the math is undeniable, and the math gets worse every year you wait.
