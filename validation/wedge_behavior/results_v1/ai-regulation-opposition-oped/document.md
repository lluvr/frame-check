## The Real Threat Isn't AI — It's the Regulation Coming for It

The current wave of AI regulation, from the EU AI Act to the proposed SAFE Innovation Framework in Washington, represents the most aggressive expansion of administrative power into a technology sector since the 1996 Telecommunications Act. And like that earlier expansion, today's regulatory enthusiasm is being driven not by demonstrable harms but by speculative ones — and the costs will be borne by the very people regulators claim to be protecting.

Start with what AI is actually doing in 2026. Models from leading labs are writing code, drafting contracts, summarizing research, accelerating drug discovery, and putting personalized tutoring within reach of any student with a smartphone. The technology is reducing the cost of expert-level cognitive work by an order of magnitude across dozens of professional domains. There are real second-order effects on labor markets and creative industries, and those deserve serious attention. But the framing of AI as an existential risk requiring preemptive regulatory containment confuses the speculative tail of capability with the actual distribution of present harm.

The EU AI Act's compliance burden is now estimated at €100,000 to €400,000 per high-risk use case, with documentation requirements that demand model cards, impact assessments, conformity reviews, and post-market monitoring infrastructure that simply does not exist in any commercially mature form. Small developers — the precise constituency that produces the next wave of innovation in any technology sector — cannot afford this overhead. The EU's own SME Council estimates that compliance costs will eliminate 60-70% of independent AI startups within their first market year.

Meanwhile, the regulations encode assumptions about AI behavior that are already obsolete. The EU AI Act's requirement that high-risk systems remain "controllable by humans" was drafted assuming agent-based AI was a future concern; the technology has moved past that frame. Static compliance frameworks for a technology evolving at this pace produce regulatory capture by incumbents who can afford the lawyers and lobbyists, not safety.

The American framing is even more incoherent. Calls for licensing AI labs above a compute threshold create a regime where only Google, Microsoft, Meta, and Anthropic can legally do research, and where every promising open-weight model becomes contraband by default. This is not safety; it is competitor protection masquerading as safety. The same regulators who would license away open research are the ones who let Boeing self-certify the 737 MAX.

The actual safety record of frontier AI in 2026 is exceptional by any reasonable comparison: zero confirmed deaths attributable to a model decision, zero major financial fraud cases pinned on autonomous AI action, zero adversarial-misuse incidents that produced harm at scale. Compare to the auto industry's first decade (40,000 deaths per year by 1929), the chemical industry's first century (Bhopal, Love Canal, asbestos), or the social media industry's first decade (mental health crisis, election interference, content moderation chaos). AI is not a mature technology, but the deployment record so far does not justify the regulatory escalation underway.

The regulators advancing these frameworks are not malicious. They are responding to genuine public concern about a technology they do not understand. But responding to legitimate concern with an inappropriate tool — heavy-handed pre-deployment regulation of a fast-moving research field — is a mistake the West can ill afford. China is not waiting for our compliance frameworks. The future of AI is being decided right now, and we are choosing to slow ourselves down for the wrong reasons.

If we want safety, we should fund interpretability research, demand transparency about training data and capability evaluations, and build adversarial-testing infrastructure that catches actual misuse. What we should not do is build the regulatory equivalent of the Maginot Line — impressive-looking, defensively positioned, and easily bypassed by anyone willing to develop where the rules don't reach.
