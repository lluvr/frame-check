# Anchor Authorship Methodology v1

Methodology for authoring lived-experience anchors per `FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §11 catalog discipline. Closes the gap between structural-grade and full-grade catalog by giving the curator a tractable, construct-honest workflow for surfacing, corroborating, and recording the lived-experience anchor required by Principle P5 (catalog grounded authorship; the primary empire moat). The methodology is reusable for cross-curator practitioners (`CROSS_CURATOR_OUTREACH_v1.md`) and for retroactive anchor authorship across the 19 retrofitted FVS entries (`data/frame_library_v3/FVS-001` through `FVS-019`).

**Author:** Lovro Lucic.
**Date:** 2026-04-26.
**Status:** v1, pre-application. Methodology proposed before the first authored anchor is shipped, so the discipline can be applied uniformly from the first attempt rather than retrofitted across a partially anchored catalog.
**Citation format:** Lucic, L. (2026). *Anchor Authorship Methodology v1.* Frame Check.
**Companion docs:** `FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` (especially §3 Layer 0 receiver state, §10.5 Principle P5, §11 catalog discipline, §13 construct-honesty plus construct-safety, §10.8 P8 honest-scope discipline); `CROSS_CURATOR_OUTREACH_v1.md` (cross-curator engagement; this methodology is the introspective protocol practitioners apply); `METHODOLOGY.md` §2.4.5 (catalog discipline integration in the canonical paper).

## 1. Purpose and scope

### 1.1 What this methodology does

The v2 spec §11 catalog discipline names eight required fields per FVS catalog entry. Seven are public-facing (authorship, context of testing, failure record, success record, lived-experience anchor, applicability metadata, empirical track record); one is internal-operational (friction-cost estimate). The 19 retrofitted entries as of 2026-04-25 are at structural-grade: all eight fields are present and operationally substantive, and the lived-experience anchor field is uniformly held "Open" with entry-specific criteria. Full-grade requires the lived-experience anchor field to be authored per entry.

This methodology is the workflow for converting "Open with criteria" to "authored anchor" without violating construct-honesty. It specifies the operating state required, the introspective protocol that surfaces anchors without confabulation, the corroboration check that distinguishes singular events from generic patterns, the format that records authored anchors, the failure-mode catalog and detection signals, the construct-honest absence outcome when no anchor is available for an entry, and the retraction protocol for anchors that turn out to be invented or mis-attributed.

### 1.2 Why anchors matter (Principle P5 brief)

Per `FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §10.5, the catalog moat against AI capability scaling is grounded authorship, not citation alone. A catalog entry with a named author, a context of testing, a failure record, a success record, applicability metadata, and an empirical track record is already disciplined beyond template-only references. The lived-experience anchor adds the irreducibly first-person element: a specific situated event the curator passed through where the entry was operative. The anchor is the layer where the entry stops being an information-processing description and becomes a curator-grounded artifact. AI systems can simulate the other fields with increasing fidelity; the anchor field, when authored honestly, is what AI systems cannot replicate without first-person agency in the curator's lived past.

Falsification path: if grounded-authorship discipline turns out to confer no measurable advantage in user trust or adoption (Prediction P6 in `FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §14.2; Claim B in §15.2), the catalog moat compresses to citation-only and the anchor field becomes academic. The methodology therefore ships before the empirical test resolves; the methodology's value is conditional on P6 holding.

### 1.3 What this methodology is not

- Not a universal theory of introspection. The protocol borrows from established traditions (micro-phenomenology, focusing, reflective practice, autoethnography) and adapts; it is not a contribution to those traditions.
- Not a template-farm. The methodology gives one workflow that applies to all 19 entries (and to future entries). It does not generate 19 parallel slot-fill templates.
- Not a substitute for first-person work. The methodology cannot author anchors on the curator's behalf; it specifies the discipline within which the curator authors them.
- Not domain-specific. The same methodology applies across curator domains (strategy, clinical psychology, sport coaching, working artistry); the entry-specific criteria adapt.
- Not a research instrument for studying introspection. The methodology is a curator-workflow tool. Treating it as a research instrument would require validation work outside this scope.

### 1.4 Honest scope (anchor authorship is bounded discipline)

The methodology is calibrated to the named scope of the v2 architecture (`FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §1): perspective shift in pragmatic problem-solving and decision-making, with enactive elements at L0 receiver state and L3.3 act-instantiation. Anchors authored per this methodology speak to the curator's lived experience of frame-divergence work in that scope. The methodology does not claim that anchor authorship validates the architecture's structural claims; that is what cross-curator stress-testing (per `CROSS_CURATOR_OUTREACH_v1.md`) is for.

### 1.5 Prioritization across the catalog

The methodology does not require authoring across all 19 retrofitted entries; the operator-time-bound environment ($1K/year normal envelope per `STRATEGY.md` §4 decision 6; operator-time as the binding constraint) makes uniform anchor coverage unrealistic. Recommended prioritization:

- **Foundational entries first.** Entries that anchor downstream concepts (e.g., FVS-001 Frame Amplification, FVS-004 Default Geometry, FVS-008 Growth Frame) carry higher leverage because their anchors compound across the catalog's interpretive frame.
- **Densest-practice entries.** Entries the curator's lived practice is densest on are candidates for early authorship; these are the entries where corroboration is highest and surfacing is least laborious.
- **Cross-curator-target entries.** Entries that a recruited cross-curator practitioner is best positioned to anchor (per their domain) are deferred to that engagement.
- **Recently-revised entries.** Phase 1C-revised entries (FVS-012, FVS-016, FVS-018) carry pre/post-revision documentation in their failure record; anchor authorship benefits from a stable post-revision entry.

Order is the curator's call. No completion deadline is part of the methodology; partial coverage with construct-honest absence on the rest is the modal expected state, not a stretch goal.

## 2. What an authored anchor is

### 2.1 The threshold from "Open" to "authored"

An entry's lived-experience anchor is authored when six conditions are jointly satisfied:

1. **Singular situated event.** A specific occurrence at a specific time with specific participants, not a pattern across multiple events. The anchor names one moment, not a tendency.
2. **First-person recall.** The curator has direct memory of the event, not reconstruction from external sources. Reading about a similar case in a book and adopting it does not satisfy this condition.
3. **Discriminant operative.** The entry's specific discriminant (the framing the entry names) was at work in the event, whether visible to the curator at the time or recognized later.
4. **Verifiable detail level.** The curator can produce details that could be checked or contradicted by external evidence: approximate dates, locations, participants, artifacts that exist (documents, messages, decisions taken). Not all details need be public; the falsifiability-in-principle matters.
5. **Outcome differential namable.** The curator can name what happened and what would plausibly have happened differently if the frame had or had not been recognized. The differential is the load-bearing element.
6. **Held within minimum-viable detail.** The authored anchor records enough to satisfy the above without exposing private detail beyond what is decision-useful for a reader. Public-facing minimum, internal-operational maximum.

Anchors that fail any of these conditions are not authored; they remain "Open" or are retired to the construct-honest absence record (§8).

### 2.2 The format that replaces the "Open" placeholder

When an authored anchor is committed, the §11 retrofit's "Lived-experience anchor" subsection is replaced. The current pattern (illustrated by FVS-008 and FVS-001) reads:

> **Lived-experience anchor.** Open. Anchor criteria for this entry: (1) ...; (2) ...; (3) ...; (4) concrete first-person recall. Held open per `FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` P5 honest-scope discipline rather than synthesized.

The authored format (per §6 below) replaces the "Open. Anchor criteria..." line with a situated description and a structured note. The format is small and decision-useful; it is not a memoir slot.

### 2.3 What an authored anchor is for, downstream

- **Citation by a reader.** A reader of the entry sees the anchor as evidence that a specific human passed through this frame, not just that a regex matched documents.
- **Cross-curator structural validity test.** A second curator's anchor on the same entry, contributed via `CROSS_CURATOR_OUTREACH_v1.md`, is the structural validity check. Anchors that diverge across curators reveal where the entry's discriminant is curator-specific rather than general.
- **Catalog evolution.** Authored anchors that later turn out to be operative under a different entry, or that surface a missing entry, drive catalog revision.
- **Construct-safety check.** Anchors are the layer at which curator self-deception is most legible. The failure-mode catalog (§7) specifies what the legible failure modes look like.

## 3. Honest-scope discipline for anchor work

Five operative principles that apply throughout the protocol. They are the construct-honesty `FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §13.1 carries into anchor work.

### 3.1 Singular events, not patterns

A pattern (recurring tendency across many events) is not an anchor. The anchor is one event. Patterns are useful for the entry's identification section and for the failure or success record (which can summarize across cases); the anchor names one. Patterns dressed up as singular events are the most common confabulation route in retrospective introspection (per Schacter's gist-vs-verbatim distinction; see §14).

### 3.2 First-person recall, not reconstruction

Recall is what the curator directly remembers. Reconstruction is what the curator infers about the past from the entry's text, from a generic schema, or from second-hand sources. Reconstructed material can be useful in other contexts; it does not satisfy anchor conditions (§2.1, condition 2). The corroboration check (§5.4) is the operating test for distinguishing the two.

### 3.3 Specific to this entry's discriminant

An anchor that fits multiple entries is not anchored to any of them. The discriminant test asks: would this anchor make sense as evidence for a different entry? If yes, the anchor is generic (FM-A1; §7.1) and either needs sharper specification or belongs under a different entry. The entry-specific criteria already attached to each "Open" placeholder are the discriminants the anchor must satisfy.

### 3.4 Falsifiable in principle

The anchor must be in-principle checkable. The curator does not have to publish the verifying details, but the curator must be able to produce them if asked. Anchors that are unfalsifiable in principle (mythicized, fully internal, no external trace) are flagged as such or held "Open" pending different anchor candidates.

### 3.5 Absence is a valid outcome

For some entries, no anchor of the required quality is available to the curator. The construct-honest response is to record the absence with a reason (§8), not to invent an anchor that fits the slot. Anchor coverage across the catalog is a function of the curator's lived practice, not a deliverable target.

## 4. The L0 prerequisite

### 4.1 Cultivation-pulled mode is the operating state required

Per `FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §3.1 dimension 2 (direction of motion), receiver state under entropy default or threat-pull is the typical baseline; cultivation-pulled mode is conscious labor. Anchor introspection done under entropy default surfaces shallow candidates that fit obvious schemas; anchor introspection done under threat-pull (hurry, performance pressure, deadline) produces fabrication or generic-pattern overlays.

The methodology's first prerequisite is operating-state cultivation. The curator does not begin the introspective protocol until cultivation-pulled mode is reached.

### 4.2 Practical setup

- **Time-bounded session.** 60 to 90 minutes is the workable range. Less than 30 minutes typically produces the surface-skim that misses anchors below the obvious schema. More than 90 minutes typically produces fatigue-driven fabrication (the curator is now searching for an anchor rather than letting one surface).
- **Single entry per session.** One FVS entry's anchor is the focus. Multiple entries in one session blur the discriminants and produce generic anchors that fit several entries (FM-A1; §7.1).
- **No mid-session research.** The curator does not consult external sources during the session. External sources can be used after the candidate surfaces (§5.4 corroboration phase) but not during surfacing or deepening.
- **No mid-session writing for publication.** Notes during the session are for the curator's process; publication-ready prose is drafted later (the same day or next day). Writing for publication during introspection collapses the descent into rhetorical performance.
- **Quiet enough.** Environmental cognitive load (notifications, deadline clock, peripheral conversation) tends to pull the curator back to threat-pull mode. The threshold is not "silent retreat"; the threshold is "the curator's attention is not being intermittently captured."

### 4.3 State-marker test

Before beginning, the curator runs a brief check: is attention narrow (defensive, hurried, performance-engaged) or broad (curious, willing to sit with not-knowing, willing to accept "no anchor available" as an outcome)? If narrow, the session is deferred. The state-marker test is not a meditation prerequisite; it is a one-question check (per Gendlin's focusing tradition; see §14). The check itself takes seconds.

### 4.4 What this is not

The L0 prerequisite is not contemplative practice, not meditation, not somatic protocol. It is an operating-state discipline borrowed in vocabulary from those traditions and calibrated to a workflow purpose. The curator does not need a meditation background to apply it. The discipline is: do not begin the protocol when the operating state would corrupt the output.

## 5. The introspective protocol

Five phases. Setup (§5.1), surface (§5.2), deepen (§5.3), corroborate (§5.4), commit-or-defer (§5.5). The protocol is adapted from Petitmengin's micro-phenomenology and Vermersch's explicitation interview (the interview-based descendants of Husserlian phenomenological reduction), Gendlin's focusing (felt-sense check), and Schön's reflection-on-action (the post-event mode of reflective practice). See §14 for citation lineage.

**Solo vs paired introspection.** Petitmengin's and Vermersch's protocols are interview-based: a trained interviewer holds the descent structure while the subject does the introspective work. Solo introspection (the curator running the protocol on themselves) is operationally harder: the same person must hold structure and descend, which loads cognitive bandwidth and increases confabulation risk. v1 of this methodology specifies solo application because the operator-time-bound environment does not yet support interviewer pairing; cross-curator engagements (`CROSS_CURATOR_OUTREACH_v1.md`) may include paired introspection (curator interviews practitioner, or a third-party interviews both) as the higher-rigor mode. Solo-application risk compounds with the §12.4 self-validation bias acknowledgment; cross-curator stress-testing is the primary mitigation.

### 5.1 Setup

1. The curator selects one FVS entry and reads its identification section plus the existing "Open" anchor criteria. The entry's discriminant is held in foreground; the rest of the catalog is set aside.
2. The state-marker test is run (§4.3). If the state is wrong, the session is deferred and rescheduled.
3. The curator opens a private working note for the session. This is not the published anchor draft; it is the trace of the session's content. Working notes remain internal-operational.
4. A timer is set for the session boundary (60 to 90 minutes).

### 5.2 Surface

The surface phase is where candidates are allowed to arise. It is not memory search.

- **Memory search vs surfacing distinction.** Memory search asks "when did this entry's frame happen to me?" and produces the most-available example fitting the entry's text. Surfacing reads the entry's discriminant, holds it without forcing recall, and notices what arises in attention. The first three to five candidates are noted without filtering.
- **What surfacing tends to look like.** A specific fragment arises (a moment, a place, a person, a phrase). The fragment is not yet a full event. The curator records the fragment in the working note without judgment ("there was a meeting where ... ; there was a Tuesday afternoon when ...").
- **What memory search tends to look like.** A generalized account assembles ("this happened many times in projects where ..."). Generalized accounts are not yet anchors. They may contain anchors as constituent events; the protocol's job is to descend to one event later. For now, generalized accounts are flagged and held aside.
- **Time budget.** 10 to 20 minutes. The phase ends when three to five candidate fragments have surfaced or the curator notices they are now searching rather than letting candidates arise.
- **Honest absence at the surface phase.** If nothing surfaces in 20 minutes, the entry's anchor is held "Open" for this session. The curator records the absence outcome in the working note. The entry can be re-attempted in a future session with different setup or different entry-context.

### 5.3 Deepen

The deepen phase takes one of the surfaced candidates (typically the most resonant, by felt-sense check) and descends into specifics. Adapted from the explicitation interview's seven structured questions (Vermersch, 1994; Petitmengin, 2006).

**Evocation, not narration.** Before descent, the curator enters an evocation state: re-living the moment from inside it rather than describing it from outside. The shift is operationally subtle (verb tense moves toward present-progressive in the curator's internal speech; sensory detail begins to surface; the curator stops summarizing and starts contacting the moment). Petitmengin (2006) names this pre-reflective access; the methodology requires a rough analog. Without evocation, the descent produces narration about the event from generic memory, which is the most common route into FM-A2 reconstructed anchor (§7.2). Time budget for evocation: 3 to 5 minutes. If the curator cannot reach evocation state on this candidate within that window, the candidate is set aside and another candidate is tried; persistent inability to evoke across multiple candidates is itself a signal that the operating-state setup is wrong (§4) or that the entry's discriminant is outside the curator's lived practice (§8).

**Sensory anchoring.** Once in evocation state, the curator surfaces sensory detail: what was visible, what was audible, what the body was doing, what posture or gesture, what objects or words were physically present. Sensory detail that surfaces without being searched for is the strongest signal of genuine first-person recall vs reconstruction. Sensory blanks where the curator has to invent are flagged as low-recall regions of the candidate; a candidate with widespread sensory blanks is at high FM-A2 risk and is held aside.

The descent covers:

- **When.** Approximate date or season; what was happening in the curator's life at that time (markers like "between project A and project B" or "the week before X event").
- **Where.** Physical location; if relevant, the institutional or organizational setting.
- **With whom.** Other participants; their roles; whether they were present at the central moment or peripheral to it.
- **Before the moment.** What was happening immediately before the event; what the curator was doing or thinking.
- **The moment itself.** The specific moment when the entry's discriminant became operative or when it failed to. What the curator perceived; what the curator was deciding or doing; what was said, written, or acted on.
- **Immediately after.** What followed; what the curator did with the moment; whether recognition was immediate or came later.
- **The discriminant in operation.** How the entry's specific frame was at work. Was it visible to the curator at the time? Was it invisible until something forced recognition? Did the curator recognize it under one frame and only later see it under the entry's frame?
- **The outcome differential.** What happened. What would have happened plausibly otherwise (with or without recognition). The differential is the load-bearing observation, not the moment itself.

The deepen phase does not require the descent to be complete. Many anchors will produce partial detail at first descent; the corroboration phase (§5.4) often surfaces gaps that drive a second descent. Time budget: 20 to 40 minutes.

### 5.4 Corroborate

The corroboration phase tests the candidate against three checks. Failing any check sends the candidate back for revision, deferment, or absence-record.

#### 5.4.1 The verifiable-detail check

Can the curator produce details that could be checked? Approximate dates, locations, participants, artifacts (emails, decisions, documents, photos, calendar entries). The curator does not have to share these; the curator must be able to produce them if asked. If the candidate cannot produce verifiable detail at any level (dates fuzzy beyond a year, no participant nameable, no artifact retrievable), the candidate is flagged as low-corroboration and either deferred or recorded as a candidate-with-low-corroboration in the working note.

#### 5.4.2 The generic-pattern check

Would this anchor make sense as evidence for a different FVS entry? The curator pictures the anchor attached to two or three other plausibly-fitting entries (the "Adjacent frames" line in the entry's identification section is a useful cue). If the anchor reads about as well under those other entries, it is generic (FM-A1; §7.1) and either needs sharper specification (more discriminant-specific detail) or belongs under a different entry. Anchors that survive this check have detail that ties them to this entry and only this entry.

#### 5.4.3 The fabrication-suspicion check

The curator asks three questions:
- Did the descent surface details I would not have predicted from the entry's text alone?
- Are there details that complicate the fit (texture that does not serve the entry's narrative)?
- Could a third party who was present recognize the description?

If all three are no, the candidate is at high fabrication risk. The candidate may still be a real event with thin recall; it may also be a generic schema dressed up as memory. The candidate is held aside; the methodology defers rather than commits at this risk level. If two of three are yes, the candidate is at moderate risk and proceeds to commit-or-defer with the risk noted in the working note. If three are yes, the candidate has standard risk and proceeds normally.

### 5.5 Commit or defer

After corroboration, one of three outcomes:

- **Commit.** The candidate satisfies §2.1 conditions 1 through 6 and survives the §5.4 checks. The curator drafts the public-facing anchor (§6 format) and updates the entry. The internal working note is filed. The catalog moves toward full-grade for this entry.
- **Defer.** The candidate has not yet met threshold but is plausibly recoverable with a second session, additional corroboration evidence (locating an artifact, confirming a date), or further descent. The curator records the candidate, the gap, and the next-attempt plan in the working note. The "Open" placeholder remains.
- **Record absence.** No candidate of viable quality has surfaced for this entry across the session (and prior sessions if any). The curator records the absence outcome per §8. The "Open" placeholder is replaced with the absence record.

The commit-or-defer rule is asymmetric on purpose: the cost of a fabricated anchor entering the catalog is higher than the cost of an entry remaining "Open" for another session. Defer is the safe action; commit is earned.

## 6. Authored anchor format

When the protocol commits an anchor, the entry's "Lived-experience anchor" subsection is rewritten. The format below replaces the "Open" placeholder; the entry-specific criteria that accompanied "Open" can be preserved in the working note (internal-operational) or removed once the anchor is authored.

### 6.1 Required public-facing fields

```
**Lived-experience anchor.** Authored {YYYY-MM-DD} per `ANCHOR_AUTHORSHIP_METHODOLOGY_v1.md`.

[Situated description: one to three short paragraphs naming the singular event,
when and where it occurred at the level of detail the curator is willing to
publish, the discriminant in operation, the outcome differential, and any
honest qualification the curator wants on the record.]

**Anchor strength.** [One short paragraph: what level of corroboration the
curator can provide if asked (artifacts, participants, contemporaneous notes),
what aspects of recall are firm vs approximate, and any qualification on the
generalizability of the single event.]
```

### 6.2 Internal-operational artifacts (not in the public entry)

- **Working note.** Session date, candidates surfaced, candidates set aside, descent details that did not enter the public anchor, fabrication-suspicion check outcomes, and commit-or-defer rationale. Working notes are retained because they are useful for retraction, revision, and methodology improvement; they are not published.
- **Corroboration trail.** Pointer to the artifacts the curator can produce on request (calendar dates, message archives, decision documents, photos). The pointer itself is internal; the artifacts are not published unless required by a specific reviewer engagement.

### 6.3 Length discipline

- **Situated description.** One to three short paragraphs. Sufficient detail for a reader to recognize the singular event; not sufficient to constitute memoir. If the description exceeds three paragraphs, the curator is likely importing material that belongs in the entry's success record or failure record rather than the anchor.
- **Anchor strength.** One paragraph. The curator's honest read on what is firm, what is approximate, what has been corroborated, and what would falsify the anchor if it surfaced.

### 6.4 What not to include in the public anchor

- Private third-party detail beyond what the third party would consent to publish.
- Identifying detail that would breach confidentiality the curator is bound by.
- Generalizations beyond the singular event (those belong in the entry's identification, success record, or failure record).
- Methodology meta-commentary (how the anchor was surfaced; that belongs in the working note).
- Speculative counterfactuals beyond the outcome differential the curator can defensibly name.

## 7. Failure modes and detection signals

Six failure modes specific to anchor authorship. Each has a detection signal and a recovery action.

### 7.1 FM-A1: Generic-pattern anchor

**Description.** A story that fits multiple FVS entries with little adjustment. The pattern is real; the anchoring is not.

**Detection signal.** The §5.4.2 generic-pattern check fails: the anchor reads about as well under two or three other plausibly-fitting entries.

**Recovery.** Sharpen the discriminant-specific detail (what was specific to THIS entry's frame) or move the candidate to the entry it actually anchors. If neither is feasible, defer or record absence.

### 7.2 FM-A2: Reconstructed anchor

**Description.** A story built from external sources (a book, a case study, a colleague's account, a generic professional schema) rather than first-person recall.

**Detection signal.** The §5.4.3 fabrication-suspicion check fails on the first question (no details surface that the curator would not have predicted from the entry's text). The descent stays at the level of the entry's identification language; nothing texture-specific arrives.

**Recovery.** Set the candidate aside. Reconstruction is useful for the entry's identification section and possibly the success or failure record; it is not an anchor. Continue surfacing in a later session.

### 7.3 FM-A3: Inflated anchor

**Description.** A real event where the curator's role or the entry's operativeness is exaggerated. The event happened; the anchor describes a more dramatic version than the curator can defend.

**Detection signal.** The curator notices, on second descent, that some details cannot be defended if challenged. The "Anchor strength" paragraph is consistently optimistic about firmness.

**Recovery.** Rewrite the situated description at the level the curator can defend. Move ambitious claims to the working note. The anchor reads more honest and is shorter; this is the right outcome.

### 7.4 FM-A4: Cherry-picked anchor

**Description.** The one event that supports the entry's framing while ignoring counter-examples in the curator's experience.

**Detection signal.** The curator can name, when asked, an event in which the entry's frame was operative but the outcome differential ran opposite. The anchor selected suppresses that counter-example.

**Recovery.** Either (a) author the anchor with the counter-example honestly named in the "Anchor strength" paragraph, (b) author both events as twin anchors if the entry permits it (some L1 axes admit multiple anchors), or (c) defer pending revision of the entry's identification or success or failure record to accommodate both directions.

### 7.5 FM-A5: Fused anchor

**Description.** Multiple events compressed into one apparently singular event.

**Detection signal.** The §5.3 deepen phase produces details from different times or different participants in apparent contradiction. Asked to specify "the moment," the curator finds two or three moments competing.

**Recovery.** Pick one of the competing moments and re-descend. The discarded moments are recorded in the working note; they may surface as anchors for adjacent entries or as additional cases in the entry's success or failure record.

### 7.6 FM-A6: Forced anchor

**Description.** No anchor is genuinely available for this entry; one is invented to fill the slot.

**Detection signal.** The §5.2 surface phase produced nothing across multiple sessions, but the curator has authored an anchor anyway, typically with low corroboration and at the level of the entry's identification language.

**Recovery.** Retract the anchor. Move to construct-honest absence (§8). The catalog improves by recording absence honestly rather than carrying invented anchors.

### 7.7 Cross-cutting detection: the cold-read test

After commit, set the anchor aside for at least a week. Re-read it without consulting the working note. Does the anchor still feel like a singular event the curator passed through, or does it now read like a generic schema? The cold-read test catches FM-A1 through FM-A6 retrospectively. Failed cold-read triggers retraction or revision (§9).

## 8. Construct-honest absence: when no anchor is available

For some entries, no anchor of the required quality is available to the curator. This is a legitimate outcome of a construct-honest catalog discipline. The recording protocol below preserves the catalog's structural-grade fields while making the absence visible.

### 8.1 When to record absence

After a minimum of two introspective sessions on the entry, separated by at least a few days, in cultivation-pulled mode each time, with the §5 protocol applied. The two-session minimum exists because a single session may produce no anchor for state reasons (the cultivation-pulled mode achieved was shallower than expected; an unrelated cognitive load was active; the candidate that would have surfaced was suppressed by the day's contents); a second session in different conditions tests whether the absence is robust to session-state variance. Three or more sessions are at the curator's discretion. If two or more sessions produce no candidate that survives §5.4 corroboration, the absence is real.

### 8.2 Format

```
**Lived-experience anchor.** No anchor authored as of {YYYY-MM-DD}. Two
introspective sessions per `ANCHOR_AUTHORSHIP_METHODOLOGY_v1.md` produced
no candidate satisfying the §2.1 threshold. Reason: [one short paragraph
naming why; see §8.3 for typical reasons].

**Status.** Open for future authorship if a candidate surfaces. The entry's
other §11 fields (failure record, success record, applicability metadata,
empirical track record) carry the entry's structural-grade discipline.
```

### 8.3 Typical reasons for absence

- The curator's lived practice has not produced an event where this entry's discriminant was operative at sufficient stake to leave first-person recall.
- The curator has lived events that fit, but none with sufficient corroboration (the curator's recall is generic-pattern level only).
- The entry's discriminant lives in domains the curator has limited direct exposure to (financial trading desk dynamics, clinical therapy session work, dance choreography), and the curator's adjacent experience does not satisfy condition 3 (discriminant operative).
- The curator has corroborated cases but they are bound by confidentiality the curator is not willing to breach for a public anchor.

### 8.4 Why absence is empire-positive

A catalog where some entries honestly carry "no anchor available" is more credible than a catalog where every entry has an anchor of mixed quality. P5 in `FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §10.5 specifies grounded authorship as the moat, not anchor-completeness. Cross-curator engagement (per `CROSS_CURATOR_OUTREACH_v1.md`) is the route by which entries with maintainer-side absence may receive anchors from practitioners whose lived practice does cover the relevant ground.

## 9. Retraction and revision protocol

### 9.1 When retraction is required

- The curator discovers (via the §7.7 cold-read test, via a fact-check, via a participant's recollection) that the authored anchor contains material the curator can no longer defend.
- A challenger produces evidence the event did not occur as described, or did not occur at all.
- Re-introspection in a later session reveals the anchor was a fused or reconstructed candidate not caught at the time.

### 9.2 Retraction format

The authored anchor is replaced by a retraction note:

```
**Lived-experience anchor.** Retracted {YYYY-MM-DD}. The anchor authored
{prior date} was found on review to {brief reason: fail cold-read; fuse multiple
events; rely on reconstruction; etc.}. Per `ANCHOR_AUTHORSHIP_METHODOLOGY_v1.md`
§9, retraction is recorded transparently rather than silently revised.

**Status.** Open for re-authorship if a different candidate surfaces. The
prior anchor's full text is preserved in `data/anchor_retractions/` for
historical record.
```

### 9.3 Revision (when retraction is too strong)

Some anchors need revision rather than retraction: a date is wrong, a participant's role is mis-described, a corroborating artifact contradicts a small detail. Revision modifies the anchor in place with a dated note. The original is preserved in working-note archives for the curator's record.

### 9.4 Empire signal of retraction

A catalog in which retractions occur is more credible than a catalog in which they do not, given that human memory and post-hoc rationalization are real. Zero retractions across an active catalog over multi-year horizon is itself a warning signal. The methodology expects some retractions; the methodology fails if retractions cannot occur transparently.

## 10. Worked illustrations

The illustrations below are about the protocol itself, not about specific FVS entries. Inventing maintainer-side anchors would violate the methodology. Each illustration uses a synthetic scenario clearly labeled as such.

### 10.1 Illustration: surface phase under cultivation-pulled mode

**Synthetic scenario.** A curator selects an entry whose discriminant is "growth-frame amplification in business analysis sessions."

The curator runs the state-marker test (broad attention, not hurried). Sets a 75-minute timer. Reads the entry's identification section once. Pauses.

Three fragments arise within twelve minutes:

1. "There was a Wednesday last autumn when I had a long session on a market sizing ..."
2. "The conversation with a colleague after the strategy memo, where I realized halfway through ..."
3. "An evening I spent rewriting a deck ... something felt off but I could not name it ..."

The curator records each fragment as one short line in the working note. None is yet a full event. The curator does not pick yet; the surface phase is for letting the candidates arrive.

**What this illustration shows.** Surfacing produces fragments, not full accounts. The curator does not start writing publishable prose at this stage. Fragments may or may not become anchors; that is the deepen phase's job.

### 10.2 Illustration: deepen phase with a corroboration failure

**Synthetic scenario continued.** The curator selects fragment 1 and descends.

When (autumn last year, approximate week unknown). Where (home study). With whom (alone with the AI session). Before the moment (had been working on the analysis for about three hours). The moment itself ("there was a point where the analysis kept refining ... I remember thinking 'this is getting really sharp' ..."). After (committed the conclusion to a memo). Discriminant in operation (growth-frame amplification across iteration; the analysis got more confident as the frame tightened). Outcome differential ("I do not actually remember whether the recommendation that came from the memo turned out well or poorly").

The corroboration check fires on §5.4.1 (verifiable detail). The curator cannot produce a memo, cannot recall the specific market, cannot retrieve the AI session log. The candidate is at low corroboration.

The §5.4.3 fabrication-suspicion check produces: did details surface I would not have predicted from the entry? (Partially; the "this is getting really sharp" feeling is texture-specific.) Are there details that complicate the fit? (Not really; the candidate is shape-consistent with the entry without resistance.) Could a third party recognize? (No; the session was solo.)

**Outcome.** Defer. The candidate is recorded in the working note as a deferred candidate. The curator notes that fragment 2 (the strategy memo conversation with a colleague) might have higher corroboration potential and is held for the next session.

**What this illustration shows.** Defer is a normal outcome. The first descended candidate is not always the anchor; corroboration failures send the curator back to the surface or to a different candidate. Defer is not a failure of the methodology; defer is the methodology working.

### 10.3 Illustration: construct-honest absence record

**Synthetic scenario.** A different curator (or the same curator, different entry) runs three introspective sessions across two weeks on an entry whose discriminant is highly specialized to a domain the curator has thin direct exposure to. No candidate surfaces past §5.2 in any session.

The curator records:

```
**Lived-experience anchor.** No anchor authored as of 2026-04-26. Three
introspective sessions per `ANCHOR_AUTHORSHIP_METHODOLOGY_v1.md` (sessions
on dates X, Y, Z) produced no candidate satisfying the §2.1 threshold.
Reason: the discriminant ([entry-specific framing]) lives in [domain] where
the curator's direct exposure is limited; adjacent experience does not satisfy
the discriminant-operative condition. Cross-curator engagement (per
`CROSS_CURATOR_OUTREACH_v1.md`) is the indicated route for an authored anchor
on this entry.

**Status.** Open for future authorship if a candidate surfaces or if a
practitioner with direct exposure contributes via cross-curator engagement.
```

**What this illustration shows.** Absence is recorded with a reason and a path forward, not silently. The catalog reader sees that absence is honest, not a gap.

## 11. Cross-curator generalization

The methodology applies to any curator. The introspective protocol (§5) is domain-agnostic; the entry-specific criteria (already attached to each "Open" placeholder) are what adapt across domains.

### 11.1 Domain examples

- **Strategy consultant.** The discriminant of an entry like "Premortem Frame" or "Stakeholder Frame" is operationally familiar; the consultant has lived events where these frames were operative under client constraints. Anchor candidates may surface from project archives more readily than for a curator without direct consulting practice.
- **Clinical psychologist.** The discriminant of L0 receiver-state entries (`FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §3.1) is operationally familiar; the psychologist has dense lived practice in observing and working with how clients receive frames. Anchor candidates may surface in session-recall mode more readily than for a curator without therapy practice.
- **Sport coach.** The discriminant of L3.3 act-instantiation entries is operationally familiar; the coach's practice is dense in events where cognitive frames were updated by lived enactment. Anchor candidates may surface in performance-review mode.
- **Working artist.** The discriminant of frame-as-perspective-shift-vehicle entries is operationally familiar; the artist's practice routinely involves frame-divergence within the work itself. Anchor candidates may surface in process-recall mode.

In each case, the §5 protocol applies as written. The corroboration check (§5.4) may need adjustment for domain-specific privacy bounds (a clinical psychologist's session detail is bound by confidentiality the methodology does not violate). The construct-honest absence outcome (§8) carries proportionally more weight in cross-curator data, since a curator with no direct exposure to the entry's domain should expect absence as the modal outcome on entries outside their practice.

### 11.2 Cross-curator engagement integration

`CROSS_CURATOR_OUTREACH_v1.md` §11 specifies a pre-execution checklist for cross-curator engagements. This methodology is the introspective protocol the practitioner applies for anchor authorship. The engagement itself does not require the practitioner to author anchors on every entry; it requires the practitioner to apply the methodology to the entries where their lived practice has bearing, and to record absence on the rest.

### 11.3 Aggregation across curators

When multiple curators have authored anchors for the same entry, the entry carries multiple anchors stacked. Anchors that diverge in their outcome differential or in which discriminant feature was load-bearing reveal where the entry's framing is curator-domain-specific rather than general. Stacked anchors with consistent outcome differential reveal where the entry's framing is robust across curators. Both signals strengthen the catalog beyond what a single-curator anchor can do.

## 12. Honest scope of this methodology

### 12.1 What this methodology rests on

The protocol borrows operationally from peer-reviewed traditions in introspective methodology (Petitmengin, Vermersch on micro-phenomenology; Hurlburt on descriptive experience sampling; Gendlin on focusing; Schön on reflective practice; Argyris on double-loop learning; Ellis and Bochner on autoethnographic discipline). The methodology adapts these traditions for a curator-workflow purpose; it is not a contribution to those traditions and does not claim to validate them.

The methodology rests on the empirical claim that disciplined first-person recall, corroborated against external evidence and screened against documented confabulation routes, can produce decision-useful anchors that pure cognition without first-person agency cannot replicate. This claim is what `FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §14.2 Prediction P6 (Claim B in §15.2) tests in the longer arc.

### 12.2 What this methodology does not validate

- Does not validate the v2 architecture's structural claims; that is what cross-curator stress-testing is for.
- Does not validate the catalog's discriminants; that is what catalog-level reliability work is for.
- Does not validate the L3.3 anchor (Prediction P5 in `FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §14.2); that is a 60-month longitudinal claim.
- Does not validate the methodology itself; the methodology's value is conditional on Prediction P6 holding.

### 12.3 Falsification path for the methodology

The methodology fails if any of the following occurs over the catalog's first 24 months of authored anchors:

- **Retraction rate exceeds initial calibration threshold.** A v1 calibration threshold of 25 percent retraction across the catalog's first 24 months of authored anchors is set as a working signal. The threshold itself is a v1 claim subject to revision after first-cycle data; a higher-than-threshold retraction rate would indicate the discipline does not catch confabulation reliably. A near-zero retraction rate over 24 months is a separate warning signal that the cold-read test (§7.7) and revision protocol (§9) are not being applied honestly.
- **Cross-curator anchor convergence below chance.** If a second curator's anchor on the same entry diverges in outcome differential or load-bearing discriminant feature in a way uncorrelated with their domain difference, the methodology is producing noise rather than signal.
- **No measurable user-facing benefit.** If readers of authored entries do not report higher trust, citability, or decision-usefulness compared to "Open" entries (the test of `FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §15.2 Claim B), the methodology's discipline is academic and the catalog moat compresses to citation-only.
- **Operator-time-cost exceeds compounding benefit.** If anchor authorship across the catalog consumes operator-time at a rate that prevents other empire activation work without producing offsetting compounding effects, the methodology is over-engineered for the operator-time-bounded environment.

Any of these triggers methodology revision; concurrent triggers may trigger retirement of the methodology in favor of citation-only discipline.

### 12.4 What success looks like

A catalog where most entries (not all) carry authored anchors that survive cold-read at one year and that one or two cross-curator practitioners can corroborate, retract, or extend. Some entries honestly carry "no anchor available" with reasoned absence records. Some entries have stacked anchors from multiple curators that reveal the entry's discriminant ranges. Retractions occur transparently and at low rate. The catalog is materially harder to replicate via citation-only or AI-generated routes than the structural-grade catalog was.

### 12.5 The AGI-era moat lever (where the methodology contributes structurally)

Per Principle P5 (`FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §10.5), the methodology contributes to the catalog moat against AI capability scaling not by raising the cost of generating anchor-shaped prose but by raising the cost of generating verifiable trace. AI systems can produce plausible anchor prose with surface details, sensory description, and structural alignment to the entry's discriminant; AI systems cannot produce calendar entries, message archives, decision documents, photos, or third-party participants whose recall could corroborate or contradict. The corroboration check (§5.4.1) and the cross-curator stress-test (§11) operate against verifiable trace, not against prose plausibility. The methodology therefore raises AI replication cost on the corroboration axis specifically, which is the axis where first-person agency in lived past has structural advantage. Prediction P5 in `FRAME_DIVERGENCE_v2.md` §14.2 is the broader bet on this asymmetry; the methodology is the operational instrument that lets the catalog accumulate evidence on which to test it.

## 13. Application checklist

A tight reference for the curator at the start of a session. The full discipline is in §1 through §12; this checklist exists to lower per-session activation cost.

**Pre-session:**
- [ ] One FVS entry selected (§5.1).
- [ ] Cultivation-pulled mode reached; state-marker test passed (§4.3).
- [ ] Quiet-enough environment confirmed (§4.2).
- [ ] 60 to 90 minute timer set (§4.2).
- [ ] Working note opened; not the published anchor draft (§5.1).

**Within the session:**
- [ ] Surface phase (§5.2): 10 to 20 minutes; three to five candidate fragments noted; no filtering.
- [ ] Deepen phase (§5.3): one candidate selected; evocation state reached; sensory anchoring surfaced; descent across when/where/with whom/before/moment/after/discriminant/outcome differential.
- [ ] Corroborate phase (§5.4): verifiable-detail check; generic-pattern check; fabrication-suspicion check.
- [ ] Commit-or-defer rule applied (§5.5): commit only if §2.1 conditions 1 to 6 jointly met and §5.4 checks survived.

**On commit:**
- [ ] Public-facing anchor drafted in §6.1 format (situated description plus anchor strength).
- [ ] Working note filed (internal-operational).
- [ ] Corroboration trail pointer recorded (internal-operational).
- [ ] Entry's "Open" placeholder replaced.

**On defer:**
- [ ] Candidate, gap, and next-attempt plan recorded in working note.
- [ ] "Open" placeholder retained.

**On absence record (after at least two sessions per §8.1):**
- [ ] §8.2 absence format applied.
- [ ] Reason stated (§8.3 typical reasons inform but do not constrain).
- [ ] "Open" placeholder replaced with absence record.

**At one week post-commit:**
- [ ] Cold-read test (§7.7) applied; revise or retract if anchor reads as generic schema.

**On retraction:**
- [ ] §9.2 retraction format applied.
- [ ] Prior anchor preserved in `data/anchor_retractions/` (created if not present).
