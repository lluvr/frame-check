Decision: Whether to refactor the OrderProcessing legacy system over the next 6 months (Option A), or wrap it with a translation adapter layer and continue building new features against the wrapper while OrderProcessing internals stay as-is (Option B).

Context: OrderProcessing is a 12-year-old monolithic service handling all order placement, invoicing, and fulfillment dispatch. Written in Python (migrated from Python 2 to Python 3 partially in 2019), uses a deprecated ORM no longer maintained, has zero unit test coverage, and any code change requires manual regression testing through QA on a 3-day cycle. Three engineers carry institutional knowledge of OrderProcessing internals; one of the three is leaving the company in 6 months. The system processes 95 percent of company revenue ($240M annual run rate); any extended downtime is catastrophic.

The Q3 product roadmap names 12 features. 8 of the 12 require OrderProcessing changes: subscription billing, multi-currency support, tax compliance overhaul, SKU consolidation, and four others. Each change currently takes 2-3 engineer-weeks per feature given the test gap and the manual regression cycle.

Option A (refactor): rewrite OrderProcessing in modern Python with full test coverage. Estimated 4 engineer-quarters of work (one of three OrderProcessing engineers full-time for 4 quarters; or three engineers for 1.5 quarters each). Risks: refactor projects have historically run 1.8x estimate on this team; team is bandwidth-blocked from new feature work during the refactor; revenue features delayed 6+ months minimum; the leaving senior engineer departs during the refactor window with institutional knowledge transfer incomplete.

Option B (adapter wrap): build a thin translation adapter exposing a clean modern interface to OrderProcessing's existing behavior; new features build against the adapter; OrderProcessing internals stay unchanged. Estimated 1 engineer-quarter (one engineer for 3 months). Risks: adapter accumulates abstraction debt; OrderProcessing remains the bottleneck for any change requiring core behavior modification; technical debt compounds across Q3 and Q4; future refactor still needed eventually (deferred not eliminated).

Stakes: 6 months of revenue feature delivery, senior engineering team morale, future refactor cost. Wrong choice on either side could cost Q3 plus Q4 product velocity. The senior engineer's departure adds time pressure: refactor starting now captures their knowledge; refactor starting after their departure costs significantly more.

Current framing: team lead is reading this as a "technical debt vs feature velocity" tradeoff. Engineering members vocally favor Option A (refactor). Product members vocally favor Option B (adapter wrap). The team lead has not yet asked which framing each position is operating from, or what the leaving engineer would do.
