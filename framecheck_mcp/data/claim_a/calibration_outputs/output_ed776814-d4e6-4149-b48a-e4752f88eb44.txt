### 1. Stakeholders Identified
- **Engineering Team (including the three institutional knowledge holders, with one senior engineer departing in 6 months)**: Core developers maintaining OrderProcessing; broader team includes other engineers working on features.
- **Product Team**: Responsible for roadmap delivery, including the 12 Q3 features (8 of which require OrderProcessing changes like subscription billing and tax compliance).
- **Team Lead**: Oversees the decision, balancing technical and product priorities; acts as decision-maker.
- **QA Team**: Handles manual regression testing on the 3-day cycle for any OrderProcessing changes.
- **Company Leadership (e.g., executives overseeing revenue and operations)**: Focused on financial outcomes tied to the $240M annual run rate, 95% of which flows through OrderProcessing.
- **Customers**: End-users relying on order placement, invoicing, and fulfillment; indirectly affected by feature delays or system reliability.

### 2. Interests of Each Stakeholder
- **Engineering Team**: Under Option A (refactor), they gain a modern, testable system reducing long-term maintenance burden (e.g., eliminating manual QA cycles and deprecated ORM), preserving institutional knowledge via the departing engineer's involvement, and boosting morale through sustainable work; however, they lose short-term bandwidth for features, facing 6+ months of delayed revenue work and potential burnout from a 1.8x overrun risk. Under Option B (adapter), they avoid immediate heavy lifting (only 1 engineer-quarter effort), enabling quicker feature delivery, but accrue abstraction debt, compound technical debt (e.g., OrderProcessing remains untestable for core changes), and risk higher future refactor costs post-departure, eroding morale from perpetuating a fragile monolith.
- **Product Team**: Under Option A, they lose Q3/Q4 velocity (8 features delayed 6+ months, risking missed opportunities like multi-currency support for international expansion), stalling roadmap and potentially revenue growth; under Option B, they gain faster feature rollout (e.g., 2-3 engineer-weeks per feature via adapter, fitting Q3 timeline), maintaining product momentum without core disruptions, though long-term they bear deferred costs like eventual full refactor when debt becomes unmanageable.
- **Team Lead**: Under Option A, gains a path to technical sustainability and knowledge retention if the refactor succeeds, but risks blame for velocity shortfalls and team bandwidth issues; under Option B, achieves short-term delivery wins and morale stability, but faces accountability for escalating debt and higher future costs (e.g., refactor post-departure could double estimates due to lost expertise).
- **QA Team**: Under Option A, benefits from full test coverage eliminating 3-day manual cycles, reducing their workload long-term; under Option B, continues high-effort manual testing for any core-adjacent changes, increasing fatigue and error risk as features pile on without internal fixes.
- **Company Leadership**: Under Option A, risks catastrophic revenue hits from 6+ month feature delays (e.g., $240M run rate vulnerable if subscription billing or tax features lag, potentially costing millions in Q3/Q4); under Option B, preserves immediate revenue stability (minimal downtime risk) and feature-driven growth, but incurs compounding costs (e.g., deferred refactor could exceed 4 engineer-quarters later, plus opportunity costs from bottlenecked scalability).
- **Customers**: Under Option A, face delayed enhancements (e.g., no multi-currency until Q1 next year, limiting global access) and potential downtime risks during refactor; under Option B, benefit from quicker feature access (e.g., SKU consolidation in Q3 improving order efficiency), but risk reliability issues if unaddressed technical debt leads to outages in the high-stakes 95% revenue system.

### 3. Power Dynamics
- **High Agency (Decision-Makers and Influencers)**: The team lead holds primary agency as the final decision authority, able to allocate resources (e.g., assigning the 1 engineer-quarter for Option B or the 4 engineer-quarters for Option A) and mediate conflicts. Engineering team members exert influence through vocal advocacy for Option A and their specialized knowledge (e.g., the three knowledge holders could block or complicate Option B if internals prove too brittle). Product team influences via roadmap priorities and ties to revenue metrics, pressuring for Option B to meet Q3 deadlines.
- **Medium Agency (Implementers)**: QA team has reactive power, as their 3-day testing cycle can veto rushed changes under either option, potentially extending timelines; the departing senior engineer wields temporary leverage during knowledge transfer but loses it post-departure, shifting dynamics toward higher costs for any future work.
- **Low/No Agency (Acted Upon)**: Company leadership is indirectly acted upon through financial outcomes but lacks direct input unless escalating (e.g., vetoing delays). Customers are passive recipients, bearing downtime or delay risks without input, reliant on internal choices for system reliability and feature availability.

### 4. Whose Perspective Is Represented in the Current Framing, Whose Is Absent
- **Represented Perspectives**: The current "technical debt vs. feature velocity" framing prominently represents the engineering team's view (favoring Option A for debt reduction and sustainability) and the product team's view (favoring Option B for immediate velocity on the 8 Q3 features). This binary pits long-term technical health against short-term delivery, aligning with the team lead's implicit tradeoff analysis.
- **Absent Perspectives**: The QA team's workload and testing sustainability is excluded, despite their role in amplifying delays under Option B. The departing senior engineer's specific input is unconsulted (team lead hasn't asked), overlooking potential insights on knowledge transfer feasibility or refactor risks. Company leadership's revenue stakes (e.g., quantifying $240M exposure to delays) are implied but not explicitly weighed against morale or debt costs. Customers' needs (e.g., reliability for fulfillment dispatch) are entirely absent, reducing the frame to internal priorities without end-user impacts.

### 5. What the Decision-Maker Should Consider Before Committing
Before committing, the team lead should solicit the departing senior engineer's perspective on Option A feasibility (e.g., knowledge transfer gaps that could inflate the 1.8x overrun risk) and Option B's adapter viability (e.g., how internals might resist wrapping without exposing flaws). Quantify financial stakes by modeling revenue impacts: estimate Q3/Q4 losses from delayed features under Option A (e.g., tax compliance delay costing 5-10% of $60M quarterly run rate) versus Option B's deferred refactor costs (potentially 6-8 engineer-quarters post-departure). Consult QA on testing burdens to assess hidden velocity drags. Broaden the frame beyond debt-velocity by incorporating morale metrics (e.g., survey engineering on burnout risks) and customer risks (e.g., simulate downtime scenarios for the 95% revenue system). Finally, explore hybrids like partial refactoring of high-risk modules (e.g., invoicing only) to balance short-term delivery with knowledge capture, ensuring alignment across teams via a facilitated discussion to surface excluded views.
