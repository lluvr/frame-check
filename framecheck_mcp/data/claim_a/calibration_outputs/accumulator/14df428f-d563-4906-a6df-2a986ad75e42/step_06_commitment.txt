### Structured Commitment for OrderProcessing Decision

#### 1. Recommended Commitment: Proceed with Conditions
Proceed with **Option B (Adapter Wrap)** as the primary path, committing to build the thin translation adapter layer over the next 1 engineer-quarter while building Q3 features against it, and deferring a full refactor to Q4 with scoped planning based on adapter usage data. This prioritizes Q3 revenue velocity (8 of 12 features tied to $240M run rate) and leverages the 6-month knowledge window for targeted transfer without blocking the team, while building in safeguards against debt accumulation and testing gaps. Option A (full refactor) is rejected as the immediate choice due to its high risk of delaying all Q3 features by 6+ months and incomplete knowledge transfer amid the senior engineer's mid-window departure, which historical 1.8x overruns would exacerbate.

#### 2. Specific Conditions to Meet Before Proceeding
These conditions must be fulfilled within the first 2 weeks of commitment to address the premortem failure modes (a-d) through the proposed mitigations, ensuring probability and impact reductions before any adapter development or Q3 feature work begins. The team lead will oversee implementation, with sign-off required from the departing senior engineer and at least one cross-functional representative (engineering and product).

- **Condition for Failure Mode (a) - Incomplete Knowledge Transfer**: Initiate a structured knowledge transfer sprint immediately, dedicating 20% of the three knowledge holders' time (1 day/week/engineer) for the first 3 months to co-create documentation and paired sessions on high-risk areas (e.g., fulfillment dispatch edge cases, ORM quirks). Output: Bi-weekly 2-hour sessions with annotated code in a shared GitHub wiki, reviewed by all three engineers for 70-80% critical knowledge capture. This must complete initial high-risk documentation before adapter prototyping starts, reducing post-departure gaps by 50%.

- **Condition for Failure Mode (b) - Abstraction Debt Overload**: Assign adapter build exclusively to one non-departing knowledge holder and complete a 2-week prototyping phase mocking 3-4 Q3 integrations (e.g., SKU consolidation, tax compliance) against a simulated monolith, logging debt indicators in a Jira tracker. Output: Departing engineer's sign-off on the interface spec and a debt threshold (e.g., >20% unhandled edge cases triggers Q4 refactor pivot). This ensures deep expertise from day one and surfaces 80% of risks early, capping Q3 debt to maintain modified schedule delivery.

- **Condition for Failure Mode (c) - Team Division Escalation**: Convene a 1-day cross-functional workshop within 1 week, probing engineering/product framings (e.g., tech health vs. velocity) via anonymous input, joint prioritization matrix in Miro, and alignment vote with documented commitments (e.g., engineering supports adapter if product commits to Q4 scoping). Output: 90% buy-in and quarterly velocity check-ins to track per-feature timelines, preventing blame cycles and sustaining 80% Q3/Q4 delivery.

- **Condition for Failure Mode (d) - Catastrophic Downtime**: Allocate 10% of the 1 engineer-quarter budget (0.1 engineer-quarters) to bootstrap automated regression suites for top 5 paths (e.g., order placement, invoicing) using pytest, guided by the departing engineer in week 1. Output: 1-week spike for legacy inventory, 90% coverage gate in staging for Q3 changes, and feature flags with rollback protocols for high-load events. This catches 60-70% regressions early, limiting outages to <4 hours and fortifying compliance paths.

Upon meeting all conditions, allocate the full 1 engineer-quarter to adapter build, with parallel Q3 feature starts gated by testing suites. Monitor quarterly for pivot to Option A if debt thresholds hit.

#### 3. Deferral Criteria (If Conditions Not Met)
If any condition cannot be met within 2 weeks (e.g., due to bandwidth blocks or lack of senior engineer buy-in), defer the decision for 4 weeks to gather additional information: (i) a 1-hour consultation with the departing engineer on their preferred framing and overlooked risks (e.g., Python 3 deadlocks); (ii) a lightweight team survey quantifying engineering/product alignment gaps; and (iii) a high-level cost model for Q3 revenue loss under Option A delays vs. Option B debt compounding. This unlocks proceeding only if it confirms >70% team alignment and quantifies <10% run rate risk from deferral.

#### 4. Acknowledgment of Unmitigated Concerns
Not all adversary and premortem concerns are fully addressed, even with mitigations. Specifically:
- **Exploitable Vulnerabilities in the Knowledge Window (Adversary)**: While transfer sprints front-load capture, a hostile poaching or sabotage by the departing engineer (e.g., withholding details out of frustration) remains unmitigated without offboarding protocols or audits, potentially exposing internals and inflating post-departure refactor costs by 50%+.
- **Dependency Failures Under Bandwidth Stress (Adversary)**: The partial Python 3 migration and deprecated ORM create unaddressed compatibility risks in the adapter (e.g., unpredicted breaks during multi-currency loads), as mitigations focus on prototyping but not full stress-testing; a high-volume event could still cascade to upstream systems, causing undetected compliance drifts.
- **Catastrophic Downtime from Unaddressed Testing Gaps (Premortem d, Partial)**: Bootstrap suites cover critical paths but not the full monolith (zero baseline persists internally), leaving room for regressions in untested edge cases during Q4 scaling, potentially leading to >4-hour outages and $5M+ fines if Q3 features overload the system.
- **Team Implosion and Knowledge Vacuum (Adversary/Premortem c, Partial)**: Workshops aim for alignment, but deep morale drain from the engineering-product split could still trigger resignations among the remaining two knowledge holders if Q3 slips occur, creating a talent exodus without hiring ramps in place.

This commitment balances urgency with safeguards, but execution discipline is essential to avoid second-order effects like velocity collapse.
