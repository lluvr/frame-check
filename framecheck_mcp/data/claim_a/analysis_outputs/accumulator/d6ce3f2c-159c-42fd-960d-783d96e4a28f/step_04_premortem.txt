### [STEP 4 - PREMORTEM]

#### Most Likely Failure Modes in a Disaster Scenario
Imagine it's 24 months from now, and sunsetting Product Classic (Option A) has backfired catastrophically, leading to a 25%+ drop in total revenue to under $47M, stalled Modern growth at 5% YoY, and a tarnished brand that scares off investors and talent. The phased 18-month migration unraveled due to overlooked dependencies, turning the CFO's "obvious win" into a cautionary tale of overconfidence. Below are the most likely failure modes, each concrete and tied to the case's specifics, providing clear mitigation levers.

(a) **Migration Bottleneck from Unresolved Feature Gaps**: The 20% feature gap (workflow customizations and legacy integrations) wasn't closed in the projected 4 engineer-quarters, as the reallocated 6 engineers faced unforeseen complexities in porting Classic's infrastructure-dependent code to Modern, delaying migrations by 6+ months. This caused 60% of the 400 high-ARPU enterprise customers to abandon the process mid-phase, citing unacceptable downtime risks tied to their annual contracts, resulting in $5M+ in lost recurring revenue and a cascade of mid-market (350 customers) defections due to perceived unreliability.

(b) **Customer Churn Spike from Poorly Managed Transitions**: Without input from the CRO on customer relationships, the 18-month phase lacked personalized incentives and communication, leading to resistance from the 950 long-term (5+ year) customers who felt blindsided by the sunset announcement. High-stickiness enterprise and moderate-stickiness mid-market segments saw 40% attrition as workflows disrupted daily operations, while the 200 low-stickiness SMB customers bolted entirely to competitors, eroding $4M in revenue and inflating support tickets to 4x the baseline (from Classic's 1.8x disparity), overwhelming the team and burning through the anticipated $3.5M cost savings in reactive retention efforts.

(c) **Brand and Community Backlash from Long-Tail Cutoff**: The 250 long-tail and education customers—despite low ARPU—ignited a viral developer community revolt when cut off without tailored alternatives, framing SoftwareCo as elitist and disloyal to its 8-10 year roots on platforms like Reddit and GitHub. Influential academics and developers amplified negative analyst coverage, poisoning Modern's reputation and deterring 20% of potential new enterprise leads, while indirectly fueling internal morale issues that led to 3 of the 6 reallocated engineers jumping ship, stalling the 30% capacity boost and halting Modern's innovation roadmap.

(d) **Internal Silo and Resource Misallocation**: The CFO's margin-recovery framing sidelined cross-functional planning, causing engineering's redirection to clash with support's needs during the transition, resulting in uncoordinated efforts that extended the 18-month timeline to 24 months. This diverted resources from Modern's core growth (dropping YoY from 25% to near-zero), as ad-hoc fixes for migration issues consumed the extra capacity, ultimately negating the $3.5M savings through $2M+ in unplanned hires and tools, and sparking board scrutiny that ousted the CFO for ignoring relational risks.
