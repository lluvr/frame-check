[STEP 3 - ADVERSARY]
### Adversarial Scenarios and Exploitation Points
From an adversary frame, sunsetting Product Classic (Option A) exposes SoftwareCo to targeted attacks by hostile actors—such as competitors, disgruntled insiders, or activist community members—who could exploit the decision's vulnerabilities to inflict outsized damage. A competitor with a similar product could launch a predatory migration campaign during the 18-month phase, poaching the 400 infrastructure-dependent enterprise customers by offering seamless legacy integration tools that undercut Modern's 80% feature parity, capitalizing on the 20% gap (workflow customizations) to promise zero-downtime switches. This is feasible because enterprise annual contracts allow renewal negotiations, and a rival could use leaked internal memos (via a hacked or insider source) to highlight the CFO's "margin-recovery" framing as evidence of neglect, eroding trust and accelerating churn to 50%+ in this high-ARPU segment. For the 250 long-tail and education users, an influential developer or academic influencer—acting as a hostile actor—could orchestrate a viral backlash on platforms like GitHub or Reddit, framing the sunset as abandonment of open-source roots, rallying the community to boycott Modern and amplify negative analyst coverage. Insiders, like a frustrated Classic engineer among the 6 being redirected, could sabotage the 4 engineer-quarters of migration work by delaying knowledge transfer, intentionally introducing bugs into the 20% feature ports that surface during enterprise testing, forcing costly fixes and exposing the 30% capacity gain as illusory.

### Catastrophic Failure Modes
Catastrophically, the sunset could trigger a revenue cliff if the 18-month phase unravels under compounded stresses, collapsing the $9M revenue stream entirely and dragging total company revenue below $50M. A key failure mode: the 950 long-term customers (5+ years) revolt en masse when the 20% feature gaps—critical for enterprise legacy integrations—aren't resolved in the promised 4 engineer-quarters, leading to a "migration mutiny" where 60%+ of the 400 enterprise users terminate contracts early, citing breach of dependency assurances, and cascade warnings to mid-market peers (350 customers), resulting in 70% attrition across high-stickiness segments. This could be exacerbated by a support meltdown: the 1.8x higher ticket rate for Classic balloons to 5x during transitions, overwhelming the team and causing multi-week outages in Modern as reallocated engineers (the 6 from Classic) struggle with unfamiliar codebases, alienating the 200 SMB users who bolt to cheaper alternatives. Brand-wise, a worst-case analyst pile-on—fueled by the unconsulted CRO's eventual whistleblowing on the CFO's narrow framing—could label SoftwareCo as "customer-hostile," tanking stock value by 20-30% and inviting regulatory scrutiny if education users claim the cutoff disrupts academic programs, turning the $3.5M cost savings into a $10M+ legal and PR nightmare. In extremis, this spirals into a death knell for Modern's 25% YoY growth, stalling at 0% as developer community exodus (sparked by the 250 long-tail influencers) poisons talent pipelines and partnership deals.

### Dependencies That Could Fail Under Stress
Under stress—such as economic downturns tightening enterprise budgets or a sudden tech talent shortage—the decision's core dependencies fracture, amplifying failure. The 4 engineer-quarters for closing the 20% feature gap relies on the 6 Classic engineers' tacit knowledge of legacy systems; stress-tested, this fails if they resist redirection (e.g., via quiet quitting or poaching by rivals), leaving Modern's team to reverse-engineer from scratch, extending timelines to 8+ quarters and derailing the 30% capacity boost. Customer migration depends on the 18-month alignment with annual contracts, but under pressure from workflow disruptions, the 400 enterprise users could invoke force majeure clauses tied to infrastructure dependencies, mass-exiting and triggering indemnity claims that drain the $3.5M savings. The CRO's absent perspective is a brittle linchpin: without it, stress from unaddressed relational risks (e.g., 8-10 year loyalty) leads to uncoordinated comms, where a single botched announcement sparks coordinated churn from mid-market (350) and SMB (200) segments, who lack the stickiness to weather confusion. Broader constraints like the 1.8x support disparity intensify under load, as a ticket surge overwhelms bandwidth, forcing hires that negate cost recoveries and expose the phased approach as a house of cards if even 20% of the 950 long-term customers defect early.

### Second-Order Effects That Could Undermine the Decision
Second-order effects ripple outward, subtly eroding the decision's foundation and creating self-reinforcing loops of decline. The CFO's margin-recovery framing, by sidelining the CRO, fosters internal silos that manifest as delayed decision-making during migration—e.g., engineering's focus on feature parity diverts from Modern's core innovations, slowing its 25% YoY growth to 10% as competitors lap SoftwareCo in new features, indirectly validating Classic holdouts' resistance. Brand risk from the 250 long-tail/education cutoff doesn't just cause immediate backlash; it undermines long-term talent attraction, with developer communities shunning SoftwareCo jobs, exacerbating the 6-engineer reallocation strain and creating a vicious cycle of delayed roadmaps. Revenue-wise, partial migrations (e.g., retaining only 50% of $9M) force reactive discounting on Modern to stem SMB churn (200 customers), compressing margins across the board and contradicting the "obvious win" narrative, potentially sparking board-level revolt against the CFO. Analyst scrutiny evolves into ecosystem effects: negative coverage deters partnerships for Modern's integrations, isolating the high-ARPU enterprise segment (400 customers) and turning the sunset's efficiency gains into isolated pockets of value amid broader stagnation, where the 8% Classic decline infects Modern's trajectory through guilt-by-association.
