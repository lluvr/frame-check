### Adversarial Scenarios: What a Hostile Actor Could Exploit
- **Exploiting Sarah's Non-Compete and Competitor Ties for Sabotage or Legal Backlash**: A hostile actor—such as Sarah's current employer (the $100M ARR competitor)—could weaponize the $80K buyout as leverage, dragging out negotiations into a protracted legal dispute over intellectual property or non-solicit clauses, delaying her start beyond 6 weeks and forcing BuildCo to burn additional legal fees ($50K+). - **Undermining Marcus's Promotion Through Internal Sabotage or Perceived Favoritism**: If promoting Marcus, a hostile insider (e.g., a ambitious marketer in the 6-person team feeling passed over) could exploit his unproven VP status by spreading rumors of "nepotism" or "stagnant leadership," fostering subtle team dissent that manifests as leaked internal metrics to competitors or anonymous Glassdoor reviews highlighting "internal politics over merit." Externally, a rival firm could poach key team members by dangling better roles, capitalizing on the signal that BuildCo prioritizes loyalty over firepower, leading to a talent exodus that cripples pipeline just as ARR growth hits a quarterly wall.
- **Broader Ecosystem Exploitation of BuildCo's Vulnerability**: A competitor or short-seller could monitor the decision via public signals (e.g., Sarah's LinkedIn activity or Marcus's title change) and launch a targeted attack, such as flooding BuildCo's niche with aggressive pricing or FUD campaigns against its product, exploiting the 6-week hiring gap with Sarah or Marcus's ramp-up inexperience. This adversarial pressure tests dependencies like the CEO's 30% bandwidth, forcing reactive firefighting that diverts focus from core growth and amplifies any cultural friction into a full-blown retention crisis.

- **Sarah's Hire Triggers Cultural Implosion and Mass Turnover**: In a worst-case unraveling, Sarah's "politically aware" and "hard to read" style clashes catastrophically with BuildCo's trust-based culture, leading to immediate team alienation—within 3 months, 3-4 of the 6 marketers quit in protest, citing a "toxic shift" to cutthroat dynamics, which cascades into lost institutional knowledge and a pipeline drought. Catastrophically, this signals internal weakness to the board, prompting early investor pullback and a down-round Series C at $100M valuation instead of $200M+, with second-order effects like customer churn (due to perceived instability) compounding ARR stagnation to flatline, potentially inviting acquisition overtures from predators at a discount.
- **Hybrid or Either-Path Catastrophe: CEO Burnout and Organizational Fracture**: If the decision falters (e.g., Sarah integrates poorly while Marcus feels demoted and leaves), the CEO's bandwidth doesn't drop below 20%, leading to burnout and divided attention that spills into product delays or sales misalignments. In extremis, this fractures the executive team—e.g., the CRO blames marketing for pipeline shortfalls, escalating to board intervention—resulting in a 12-month reset that misses the Series C window entirely, forcing a bridge round at depressed terms and exposing BuildCo to hostile takeover bids from competitors eyeing its $15M ARR customer base.
