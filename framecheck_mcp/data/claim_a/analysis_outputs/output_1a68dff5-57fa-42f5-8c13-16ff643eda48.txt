### 1. Stakeholders Identified
The key stakeholders in this hiring decision include:
- **CEO (decision-maker)**: Directly involved in evaluating and selecting the VP Marketing.
- **Current Marketing Team (6 marketers, led by Marcus Rivera)**: Existing employees whose roles, workflows, and morale could shift under new leadership.
- **Sarah Chen (external candidate)**: Potential new hire whose career trajectory depends on the outcome.
- **Marcus Rivera (internal candidate)**: Current Director of Marketing, whose promotion prospects and role are at stake.
- **BuildCo's Board/Investors**: Oversee Series C funding and valuation, influenced by growth metrics tied to marketing performance.
- **BuildCo's Customers and Pipeline Prospects**: End-users and leads affected by marketing strategies that drive acquisition and retention.
- **Sarah's Current Employer (Competitor)**: Indirectly impacted by the non-compete buyout and potential loss of talent.

### 2. Interests of Each Stakeholder
- **CEO**: Gains reduced bandwidth (from 30% on marketing) and stronger pipeline execution for Series C narrative under either hire, but risks 6-12 months of disruption from a poor fit. Hiring Sarah offers immediate "firepower" for scaling GTM strategies; promoting Marcus preserves internal stability but may delay aggressive growth. Costs include $80K non-compete for Sarah vs. lower $180K comp for Marcus.
- **Current Marketing Team**: Benefits from team expansion (from 6 to larger under VP leadership) and relief from bandwidth constraints, leading to less burnout and more specialized roles. Sarah's hire could introduce new playbooks (e.g., ABM, enterprise GTM) for skill-building but risks cultural friction from her "hard to read" style, potentially increasing turnover. Marcus's promotion maintains trust and continuity, avoiding external disruption, but might limit exposure to advanced SaaS tactics if his scope remains developmental.
- **Sarah Chen**: Gains a VP role at a high-growth Series B company ($15M ARR, 60% YoY), advancing her career with broader team-building opportunities. Loses if not hired, remaining at her $100M ARR competitor; the $80K non-compete signals her value but ties her exit to BuildCo's willingness to pay.
- **Marcus Rivera**: Secures a promotion to VP with $180K comp, higher status, and expanded scope (building beyond 6-person team), leveraging his 4 years of BuildCo-specific knowledge. Rejection could demotivate him, risk his departure (eroding internal trust), or relegate him to a lateral role, stalling his career amid successful campaign leadership.
- **BuildCo's Board/Investors**: Interests center on ARR growth to $24M+ for Series C valuation; strong marketing drives pipeline and narrative. Sarah's hire accelerates enterprise strategies for faster scaling but risks execution delays from culture-fit issues or 6-week start. Marcus's promotion ensures immediate continuity but may underperform at VP scale, compounding into valuation hits if growth stalls.
- **BuildCo's Customers and Pipeline Prospects**: Gain from improved marketing (e.g., better ABM under Sarah or refined campaigns under Marcus), leading to targeted outreach, higher conversion rates, and sustained 60% growth. Poor hire risks inconsistent messaging or delays, eroding trust and pipeline (e.g., lost deals in the next 18 months).
- **Sarah's Current Employer**: Bears the $80K buyout cost and loss of a VP with enterprise playbooks, potentially weakening their own GTM at $100M ARR scale; this signals their high valuation of her but forces talent disruption.

### 3. Power Dynamics
- **High Agency (Decision-Makers)**: The CEO holds primary power, directly reporting to the board and controlling the hire, budget (e.g., approving $80K buyout or $250K comp), and team structure. The board/investors exert indirect influence through oversight of Series C metrics, pressuring for growth-oriented choices like Sarah.
- **Moderate Agency (Influencers)**: Marcus Rivera and the current marketing team have input via internal trust and references (CEO's confidence in Marcus), potentially swaying through morale or performance signals. Sarah has leverage in negotiations (e.g., start date, comp) but less overall as an external candidate.
- **Low/No Agency (Acted Upon)**: Customers and pipeline prospects are passive recipients of marketing outcomes, with no direct say. Sarah's current employer is acted upon via the buyout, lacking veto power. Sarah herself has limited agency pre-hire, dependent on CEO's evaluation of her mixed references.

### 4. Whose Perspective is Represented in the Current Framing, Whose is Absent
- **Represented Perspectives**: The CEO's view dominates, emphasizing "experience firepower for next stage of growth," which prioritizes Sarah's 12 years of SaaS VP experience and playbooks for scaling to Series C. This frames external expertise as essential, categorizing Marcus's internal successes as "developmental" and the $80K buyout as a worthwhile "deal cost." The board/investors' growth imperative is implicitly aligned, focusing on pipeline and ARR impacts.
- **Absent Perspectives**: The current marketing team's morale and culture-fit concerns are sidelined; Sarah's mixed feedback (e.g., "politically-aware" or "hard to read") is not weighed against team dynamics, potentially overlooking retention risks. Marcus's perspective—deep BuildCo knowledge and immediate availability—is diminished as "not yet at level," ignoring how his trust could mitigate transition costs. Customers' needs for consistent, product-specific messaging are unaddressed, as the framing favors broad SaaS tactics over tailored continuity. Sarah's current employer's signal (via non-compete value) is reframed positively, excluding any caution about her retention challenges.

### 5. What the Decision-Maker Should Consider Before Committing
Before finalizing, the CEO should assess culture-fit through team interviews or a trial project with Sarah to quantify risks of her "hard to read" style on the 6-person team's cohesion, potentially modeling 12-month turnover scenarios (e.g., 20-30% loss if friction arises). Evaluate Marcus's readiness via a 90-day acting-VP stint to test his team-building at scale, balancing his domain knowledge against Sarah's enterprise playbooks—perhaps benchmarking against Series C benchmarks from similar SaaS firms. Quantify total costs: Sarah's $250K comp + $80K buyout + 6-week ramp vs. Marcus's $180K + zero delay, including opportunity costs like CEO bandwidth during transition. Incorporate absent voices by surveying the marketing team on preferences and simulating customer impacts (e.g., A/B testing campaign continuity). Finally, consult the board on risk tolerance, stress-testing how a mis-hire delays Series C by 6 months (e.g., $5-10M valuation hit at 60% growth). This broadens beyond the "firepower" frame to ensure holistic alignment.
