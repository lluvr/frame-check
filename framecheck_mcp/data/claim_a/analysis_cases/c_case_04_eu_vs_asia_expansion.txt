Decision: Whether to enter the European market (UK, Germany, France) over the next 18 months, enter the Southeast Asia market (Singapore, Indonesia, Vietnam) over the next 18 months, or defer international expansion entirely and double down on US growth.

Context: SaaSCo is at $40M ARR, US-only, growing 50 percent year-over-year. The board is pushing international expansion as a Series C narrative. International expansion would consume 25-30 percent of company resources for 12-18 months.

EU specifics:
- Total addressable market: $400M (mature, established competitors in each country).
- Regulatory complexity: GDPR compliance work estimated at 2 engineer-quarters; data residency requirements add infrastructure cost.
- Localization: 3 languages, 3 sales geographies; estimated 18 sales hires.
- Customer acquisition cost: 1.4x US benchmarks (more competitors).
- Deal sizes: 1.2x US (larger enterprise customers).
- Timeline to first $5M ARR: 14-18 months.

SEA specifics:
- Total addressable market: $180M (less mature, fewer entrenched competitors but smaller per-customer revenue).
- Regulatory: lighter (Singapore is the legal HQ; Indonesia and Vietnam each have specific data laws but less stringent).
- Localization: 2 languages plus Singapore-English, 3 sales geographies; estimated 12 sales hires.
- Customer acquisition cost: 0.8x US benchmarks (less competition).
- Deal sizes: 0.6x US (smaller enterprise customers, mid-market dominant).
- Timeline to first $5M ARR: 12-15 months.

US double-down alternative: invest the 25-30 percent of company resources into deeper US enterprise penetration. Estimated additional $8-12M ARR from US-only investment over the same 18 months.

Stakes: $5-10M of incremental international ARR over 18 months vs $8-12M of US ARR over the same period; Series C narrative ("we are international" vs "we are dominant in the US"); 25-30 percent of company resources committed for 18 months; international ops complexity has historical 1.5x cost overrun on this team.

Current framing: CEO is reading this through "Series C narrative requires international" frame; the board's expectation is dominant. The US-double-down alternative is treated as "the boring option" rather than as an honest comparison. The choice between EU and SEA is being framed as "where the revenue is bigger" rather than as a strategic positioning question.
