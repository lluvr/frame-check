Decision: Whether to build a deep integration with DominantPlatform (which owns 60 percent of the relevant distribution channel) on their standard partner terms, build a shallow integration that preserves multi-platform optionality, or refuse the integration entirely.

Context: ContentCo is a $25M ARR content-management platform. DominantPlatform's app store is the primary distribution channel for ContentCo's target market (creators with 10K-1M followers). ContentCo currently has 45 percent of its customers acquired through DominantPlatform's app store; the remaining 55 percent is direct plus small alternative platforms.

DominantPlatform's deep-integration terms:
- Promoted placement in app store ("Featured" category for 18 months).
- Access to DominantPlatform's user data API (analytics on creator behavior).
- 18 percent revenue share to DominantPlatform on customer LTV (vs current 10 percent).
- Exclusivity covenant: ContentCo's deep integration must not be replicated on the two top alternative platforms for 24 months.
- DominantPlatform reviews and approves all major UI changes for the integration.
- 24-month minimum commitment.

Shallow integration alternative: standard app-store integration; no exclusivity; no preferred placement; standard 10 percent revenue share. ContentCo can build similar integrations on alternative platforms.

Refuse alternative: ContentCo invests in direct customer acquisition plus alternative platform diversification. Estimated 50 percent reduction in DominantPlatform-sourced customers within 12 months.

Stakes: 45 percent of customer acquisition vs strategic-optionality on alternative platforms for 24 months. DominantPlatform has historically used exclusivity covenants to consolidate market power (industry analysis suggests 70 percent of partners with similar deals see their businesses become DominantPlatform-dependent within 36 months). The 18 percent revenue share over 24 months is approximately $4M.

Current framing: ContentCo's COO is reading this through "necessity, the platform is too dominant to refuse" frame; the deep-integration option is treated as the default with minor terms negotiation. The 60-percent-distribution figure is taken as given and the question is framed as "how to capture the most of that 60 percent" rather than as "what is the cost of being captured by the 60 percent."
