Decision: Whether to begin sunsetting Product Classic over 18 months (Option A: phased migration of customers to Product Modern, eventual decommission), continue investing in Product Classic at maintenance level (Option B: stable but no new features), or consolidate Product Classic features into Product Modern (Option C: feature-parity migration without sunset).

Context: SoftwareCo has two products: Product Modern (launched 2022, 70 percent of revenue, 25 percent YoY growth) and Product Classic (launched 2014, 30 percent of revenue, 8 percent YoY decline). Product Classic has 1,200 customers; 950 of those are 5+ year customers paying annual contracts. Product Classic's customer base is segmented:
- 400 enterprise (high ARPU, high stickiness, infrastructure-dependent).
- 350 mid-market (mid ARPU, moderate stickiness, some workflow dependencies).
- 200 SMB (low ARPU, low stickiness, mostly using basic features).
- 250 long-tail and education (very low ARPU, high social cost if cut).

Product Classic's engineering load: 6 of 22 engineers maintain Product Classic full-time. Customer support handles a higher per-customer ticket rate for Classic than Modern (1.8x). Combined fully-loaded cost of Product Classic operation: ~$3.5M per year against $9M per year revenue.

Product Modern can replicate ~80 percent of Product Classic's features today; the remaining 20 percent (workflow customizations, legacy integrations) would require 4 engineer-quarters of dedicated work to migrate.

Stakes: $9M revenue from Product Classic vs $3.5M operational cost; engineering capacity (6 engineers redirected could add 30 percent capacity to Product Modern roadmap); customer relationships including some 8-10 year customers; brand risk from sunset announcement (industry analysts watch for this).

Current framing: CFO is reading this as "the data is clear; Product Classic margin is bad and trending down; sunset frees engineering capacity." The CFO has framed it as a margin-recovery decision and has presented the case as "this is what we should obviously do." The CRO has not been asked yet for the customer-relationship perspective. The 250 long-tail and education customers (many of which are influential in academic and developer communities) are not currently in the framing.
