Decision: Whether to commit to remote-only work policy permanently (Option A), maintain current hybrid policy with 2-day-per-week office expectation (Option B), or shift to office-default policy with limited remote (Option C).

Context: BuildSoft is a 180-person engineering-heavy company. The current hybrid policy was implemented in 2022 (post-pandemic) and is up for renewal. Current state:
- 60 percent of staff in 2 office locations (HQ in San Francisco; engineering hub in Austin).
- 40 percent fully remote across 18 US states and 3 countries.
- Office attendance under hybrid policy: average 1.4 days per week (below the 2-day expectation).
- Engineering productivity (story points completed): 12 percent higher in remote-heavy teams.
- Cross-functional collaboration metrics (Slack response time, decision velocity): 18 percent worse in remote-heavy teams.
- Recent senior hires (last 12 months): 65 percent prefer remote, 25 percent prefer hybrid, 10 percent prefer office.

Office lease costs: HQ ($2.4M per year, lease ends in 8 months; renewal would be $2.6M); Austin ($800K per year, 18 months left). Renewing both costs $3.4M per year ongoing.

Talent dynamics: competitors in BuildSoft's space have moved to remote-only (3 of 5 closest competitors); 2 maintain hybrid. BuildSoft's hiring funnel shows 70 percent of senior candidates filter for remote; loss rate to remote-first competitors is 40 percent on senior offers.

Stakes: $3.4M per year in office cost; talent acquisition and retention; engineering productivity vs cross-functional velocity; existing employees' lifestyle expectations (some moved cities for the hybrid policy); brand identity (BuildSoft has historically marketed itself as "in-person engineering culture").

Current framing: HR Director is reading this through "competitive talent demands remote" frame; the office cost and talent loss numbers are dominant in the framing. The cross-functional collaboration data is treated as "we will figure out tooling" rather than as a load-bearing constraint. The brand-identity question is treated as "marketing can adapt" rather than as a substantive question. The existing-employee expectations (people who moved cities for hybrid) are not currently in the framing.
