Short answer: it can be a great move at 35 with a finance background—but only if you de‑risk the big unknowns before you resign. Fintech carries extra complexity (regulatory, fraud, bank partners) on top of the usual startup risks, so your edge and your runway matter a lot.

A quick framework to decide
- Unique edge: Do you have a specific problem you’ve lived with, proprietary insight, or distribution advantage (access to customers, data, or a partner) that others don’t?
- Runway: Can you cover 18–24 months of living costs and basic startup burn without income? If not, plan to extend runway or validate more before quitting.
- Early validation: Have you done 30–50 conversations with target users and secured 3–5 LOIs or pilot commitments? Ideally with willingness to pay or clear ROI.
- Go-to-market: Do you have a credible path to acquire your first 10–20 customers? Partnerships, a defined vertical niche, or a sales motion you understand?
- Compliance path: Do you know the regulatory regime you’ll operate under and how you’ll handle it (in-house compliance lead, outside counsel, sponsor bank/BaaS, licenses)?
- Team: Do you have (or can you quickly attract) a complementary cofounder covering product/engineering or compliance/risk, depending on your strengths?
- Personal fit: Family/support system onboard? You’re comfortable with high uncertainty and the career narrative if you need to re-enter finance later.

De-risking steps before you quit
1) Check constraints
- Review employment contract for non-compete, non-solicit, inventions assignment, and moonlighting policies. If you’re registered (e.g., FINRA), understand restrictions on outside business activities and notice periods.

2) Validate the problem and demand
- Do 30–50 structured customer interviews. Focus on painful, frequent, budgeted problems and the current workaround cost.
- Define a tight beachhead (one vertical, one job-to-be-done).
- Aim for 3–5 LOIs or pilot agreements with success criteria and target pricing.

3) Prototype and pre-sell
- Build a clickable demo or scrappy MVP (no-code/low-code where possible).
- Stand up a landing page and capture interest. Test a pricing page or pilot offer.
- For B2B, try to secure design partners willing to commit time/data and a path to paid use.

4) Map compliance and partners early
- Identify which regimes apply: payments/money transmission, lending, wealth/broker-dealer, KYC/AML, PCI, data privacy.
- Decide “build license vs. partner.” Many early fintechs partner with a sponsor bank or compliant platform first; diligence their regulatory track record and SLAs, and avoid single‑point dependency where possible.
- Budget for legal and compliance from day one; get outside counsel scoped.

5) Set go/no-go criteria and timelines
- Example 60–90 day targets: 40 interviews, 4 LOIs, 2 pilots lined up, clear compliance path, a simple MVP, and a distribution hypothesis that pencils out.

If you go full-time, a focused 90-day plan
- Incorporate (typically DE C‑Corp), founder vesting with 83(b), basic IP assignments, and an option pool. Open bank accounts and set up lightweight accounting.
- Security/compliance basics: data handling plan, vendor risk reviews, least-privilege access, incident response, audit trails; start a SOC 2 path if you’ll sell to enterprises.
- Lock a sponsor bank/BaaS or other critical infra with clear responsibilities (KYC/AML ownership, dispute handling, funds flow, and uptime).
- Build a narrow wedge MVP that solves one acute problem; instrument it from day one.
- GTM: pick a single channel (vertical partnerships, founder-led sales, or PLG) and set weekly activity and conversion targets.
- Metrics to watch early: time-to-value for first users, conversion to paid, gross margin/unit economics, sales cycle length, fraud/chargeback rates (if moving money).

Common fintech pitfalls to avoid
- Underestimating compliance and fraud costs; one bad incident can sink you.
- Relying on a single bank/BaaS partner without contingency planning.
- Going too broad; pick a vertical and nail one workflow first.
- Building before validating willingness to pay and procurement hurdles.
- Ignoring data privacy and security until late—enterprise buyers will block you.

Alternatives to an all-in jump
- Ask for a sabbatical or reduced schedule to extend runway.
- Validate as a side project (if allowed) until you have pilots/LOIs.
- Join an early-stage fintech as a founding PM/BD/Head of X to learn and de-risk while building network and insight.
