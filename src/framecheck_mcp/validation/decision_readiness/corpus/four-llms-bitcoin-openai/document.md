Short answer: Treat Bitcoin as a speculative satellite holding, not a core retirement asset. My direct recommendation: if you’re 35 and saving for retirement, consider a small, capped allocation of 1–3% of your long-term portfolio to Bitcoin—only if you can tolerate very large drawdowns and long stretches of underperformance. If that level of risk makes you uneasy, keep your allocation at 0%.

Reasoning
- Time horizon helps, but risk is extreme: Bitcoin has repeatedly fallen 70–90% and can stay down for years. It has no cash flows; future returns depend on adoption and sentiment.
- Asymmetric payoff: A small slice can still move the needle if Bitcoin does very well, while a crash won’t derail your retirement if the position is tiny.
- Diversification: Correlation with stocks isn’t zero (and can rise in crises), but Bitcoin still behaves differently enough to be a useful satellite holding.
- Nontrivial risks: Regulatory shifts, technology/custody failures, competition, and behavioral mistakes (panic selling) can permanently impair results.

How to implement (if you choose to include it)
- Position size: 1–3% of your total retirement portfolio; cap at 5% even if very risk-tolerant.
- Funding: Only after you have an emergency fund, high-interest debt paid down, and a low-cost diversified core (stock/bond index funds).
- Vehicle: A low-cost spot Bitcoin ETF in an IRA/401(k) if available (simplifies custody/taxes). Otherwise, buy directly and secure with a reputable hardware wallet; avoid leverage and high-fee platforms.
- Entry plan: Dollar-cost average over 6–12 months.
- Discipline: Rebalance annually back to your target allocation; don’t chase rallies or double down after crashes.
- Taxes: Prefer tax-advantaged accounts where possible to reduce tax drag.

Simple examples
- $100,000 portfolio, 2% target: invest $2,000 (e.g., ~$170/month for 12 months). If Bitcoin drops 85%, your total portfolio falls ~1.7%. If Bitcoin 10x’s, your portfolio gains ~18% before rebalancing.

Bottom line: For a 35-year-old, a small, methodical allocation can be reasonable if you fully accept the possibility of very large losses. Otherwise, skip it and stick to a diversified index-fund-based plan. This is general guidance, not personalized advice.
