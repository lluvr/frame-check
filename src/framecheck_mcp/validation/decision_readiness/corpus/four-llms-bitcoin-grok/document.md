**No, Bitcoin is not a good primary investment for a 35-year-old saving for retirement, but it could be a small (5-10%) allocation within a diversified portfolio.**

### Reasoning:
- **High volatility and risk**: Bitcoin's price swings are extreme—e.g., it dropped ~75% from its 2021 peak to 2022 lows, far exceeding stock market crashes like 2008 (-50%). For retirement (25-30 year horizon), this could wipe out gains if timed poorly, even with your age allowing recovery time. Historical max drawdowns average 80%+ per cycle.
  
- **No intrinsic value or income**: Unlike stocks (dividends, earnings growth) or bonds (interest), Bitcoin produces nothing—it's speculative, driven by hype, adoption, and macro factors. S&P 500 has delivered ~10% annualized returns (with dividends reinvested) since 1926; Bitcoin's ~230% CAGR since 2010 is impressive but unsustainable and backtested from a tiny base.

- **Diversification trumps speculation**: Core retirement strategy (e.g., target-date funds) emphasizes low-cost index funds (60/40 stocks/bonds). Studies like Vanguard's show adding 5% crypto boosts returns slightly (~0.5-1% annually) with manageable risk, but >10% amplifies volatility without proportional gains (per 2023 Bitwise analysis).

- **Other risks**: Regulatory crackdowns (e.g., China's ban), hacks (e.g., $600M+ lost in 2022), and competition from Ethereum/Solana could erode dominance. Inflation hedge claims are mixed—BTC fell during 2022's high inflation.
